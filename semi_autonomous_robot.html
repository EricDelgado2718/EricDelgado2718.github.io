2.007 Robot Build Challenge
Goal Chassis and Drivetrain Boom Lift Roller Spinner Final Robot

Goal

  As part of 2.007, I was tasked with designing and fabricating a remote controlled robot to traverse a competition field, perform tasks to score points, and compete against other students.

  The goal of the class was to present concepts, principles, and algorithmic foundations for robots operating in the physical world, and implement these in a competetive setting.

Chassis and Drivetrain

  One of the main concerns when designing the chassis was ensuring a rigid foundation while minimizing mass and having enough attachment points to place all the subcomponents. To achieve this, I waterjetted four horizontal slats with tenons on the ends of them, and two horizontal slats with mortices. The two middle slats were slightly longer to make the fit an interference fit - and the edge slats were secured using screws. This resulted in a sturdy, rigid base upon which I could continue to build my robot.

  For my drivetrain, I opted for two actuated front wheels and two caster for the rear wheels. I found if I used normal, passive wheels for the rear, there was a lot of sliding friction when trying to turn - a problem that was circumvented by the casters. To power the front wheels I used two Go-Bilda 25-2 Torque Motors. This chassis and drivetrain setup ended up being quite agile and maneuverable.

Boom Lift

  The total space constraint of all robots upon starting a round was 12"x12"x18". Many of the tasks, though, required a reach much longer than 18", some requiring an end affector to reach 30" above the ground. To satisfy this, I designed an lifting mechanism inspired by a boom lift. It consists of four linkages bent out of sheet metal, connected by delrin rods, and extended by a string routed through pulleys connected to the linkages.

  The boom lift was attached to the chassis through a pin joint located at the base of the boom lift and actuated by a Go-Bilda 25-2. This could rotate and hold the boom lift at every position between horizontal and vertical. To extend the boom lift, I designed a winch system actuated by an LDO-25GM310 motor, attached to the base of the boom lift. To retract the boom lift, the winch would release extra string and the base motor would orient the boom lift vertically for gravity to pull the boom lift down.

Roller

  The Roller Mechanism was an end affector designed to extract plastic tubes held in a shelf-like container. To remove the plastic tubes, I designed an aluminum bracket to hold in a delrin roller, and wrapped the outside of the delrin roller with the sticky part of duct tape. I applied torque to the delrin roller using a chain and sprocket actuated by an LDO-25GM310. The roller was then rigidly attached to the furthest linkage of the boom lift.

  To use the roller, the robot would drive up and push the roller up against the plastic tubes. At this point, the roller would spin backwards, driving the tubes out of their slots.

Spinner

  The spinning mechanism is meant to raise a elevator platform by turning a crank attatched to a raising platform that the robot stands on - hence why minimizing weight is so important for effective operation. The crank is spun by a windmill-esque feature with four proturing screws meant to catch the features on the crank - this spinner is driven by two LDO-25GM310 motors in a chain drive with a 2:5 gear ratio.

  To spec both the motor torques and the gear ratio, I measured the torque required to spin the crank, found the torque-speed curves for the motors in conjunction, and chose motors that would let me achieve that torque at optimal power output.

Final Robot

  Every component on the final robot worked as intended and performed well on the course. During a competition, though, I quickly found out that sometimes less is more. I found I had too many different inputs I needed to control at the same time - ie: driving, extending boom lift, pitching roller backwards, and getting the optimal boom lift angle all simultaneously to remove the plastic tubes from the shelf. This many manual operations proved to ok while testing in a controlled environment, but ultimately led to my downfall in the competition.

  If I had to go back and redesign, I would have constrained the boom lift to be set at a certain angle and have a toggle switch to turn the roller on and off (basically remove as many degrees of freedom as possible without sacrificing performance).
