Bionic Hand
Contributors: Elizabeth Brennan, Charlotte Pradel
Goal Tendon Routing Damper Controls Final Product

Goal

  The goal of this project was to design and fabricate a fully-determined (same number of actuators than DOFs) bionic hand, building off of a previous design of an over-determined bionic finger.

  The physical design component was assigned to fellow student Elizabeth Brennan, while I was tasked with rerouting the tendons to maximize maneuverability while minimizing number of tendons, as well as designing and implementing the control framework for the hand.

Tendon Routing

  Given that our bionic hand was to be a tendon-driven system due to it's small size, I needed to control the fingers using tendons and could thus only apply a torque in one direction. This is normally solved with implementing an agonist-antagonist muscle structure, but given my constraint of having maximally 4 tendons per finger, I could not implement this without being limited to two degrees of freedom.

  With some thought-out routing, though, I was able to implement a routing scheme that would allow us to control 3 DOFs bidirectionally and one DOF unidirectionally (index finger normally has 4 DOFs) using co-contraction. The fingers designed by Elizabeth featured a ball joint at the MCP joint, and hinge joints at the PIP and DIP joints. The three bidirectional DOFs were MCP ab/adduction and flextion/extension, as well as PIP flexion/extension. The unidirectionally controlled joint fell to DIP flexion. I opted for flexion of this joint over extension due to the usefulness of "hook" and "cylindrical" grips, at the expense of performance in the "pinch" grip (possible but not as strong with DIP flexed).

Damper Design

  After doing research with regards to fully determined systems, I found that a failure to allow for some "give" will ofterntimes lead to a failed movement. Therefore, I designed a small damping mechanism to insert between the motor and the tendon attachment, giving the tendon an artificial stretch as well as a damped passive return to the neutral state. Modifying the spring stiffnesses would allow one to modulate the tendon stiffness and biases accordingly. The also adds some flexibility to the fingers.

  Pulling on the tendon (actuation from the motor), would compress the spring and allow the damping material to extend. Upon release, the damping material would resist the force of the spring for a slower, more controlled return to neutral without oscillation.

Controls

  To control the seven motors of the bionic finger we were to take inspiration from, my supervisor Charlotte Pradel used two arduinos in a "master" and "slave" node configuration communicating over I2C. The problem presented to me at the beginning of the project, was that we now needed to control 16 motors, and we wanted all the fingers to be controlled simultaneously - I needed to implement a communication protocol that was capable of parallel communication, and I ultimately landed upon CAN bus.

  To have enough PWM pins to control every stepper motor, I ended up using an Arduino Nano as my master node and an Arduino Uno and three Arduino Megas as my slave nodes. I used five MCP2515 modules connected with twisted 20AWG wires to communicate between the Arduinos.

  The Arduino Nano computes the joint angles for each finger given fingertip position using inverse kinematics, and sends this information through the bus. The first bit in every message is an ID tag, which corresponds with a slave Arduino. All slave Arduinos listen for the messages passed through the bus, and if their ID corresponds to the first bit in the message, the Arduino will accept the message. Based on the command given by the master Nano, the receiving Arduino will actuate each motor by a specified number of turns, resulting in actuation of the tendon and the hand reaching the goal position.

Final Product

  The final product consists of 16 motors actuating 4 digits assembled atop a LEGO assembly. The tendons are routed as described above and each tendon has an associated damper for artificial stretch.

  To test the feasibility of this prototype, certain hand positions were hard coded into the master Arduino, but the hope for the future was to continue development to eventually take EMG as the input to the control scheme.

  The final product was capable of full actuation in 12 degrees of freedom and half-actuation in 4 degrees of freedom.
